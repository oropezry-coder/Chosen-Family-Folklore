Chosen Family as Contemporary Folklore

Exploring LGBTQ+ Rituals, Identity, and Community

Home Research Article Summary Bibliography

Abstract

Shared meals such as Friendsgiving help create belonging in chosen families.

This project examines chosen family in LGBTQ+ communities as contemporary folklore. It focuses on informal rituals such as Friendsgiving, coming-out celebrations, and name-change gatherings that create belonging. These traditions are learned through participation, repeated, and adapted over time. By analyzing their forms, meanings, and contexts, this research shows how chosen-family practices reflect values of care, resilience, and identity while redefining kinship and building community.

Research

Rituals (Formal Aspects)

Chosen-family rituals function as customary folklore because they are informal, repeated, and meaningful within a group. Events such as Friendsgiving, coming-out celebrations, and transition gatherings involve shared meals, symbolic acts, and affirmations. These repeated structures give rituals stability while allowing flexibility and creativity (Weston 1991).

Community gatherings reinforce identity and shared values.

Context (Where and When)

These rituals occur in homes, LGBTQ+ community centers, and digital spaces. These environments shape how traditions are experienced and show how chosen-family practices adapt across different settings. These spaces are especially important when traditional family environments are not supportive (Browne 2006).

Language and Storytelling

Language helps define chosen-family identity. Terms such as “chosen family” and “community” create meaning and belonging. Storytelling, including sharing personal experiences like coming out, reinforces collective identity. These stories function as verbal folklore, where meaning is created through interaction and performance (Bauman 1977).

Article Summary

Kath Weston’s Families We Choose explains how LGBTQ+ individuals construct alternative kinship systems outside traditional biological frameworks. These chosen families are built through shared experience, emotional labor, and repeated social practices. Rather than replacing biological families, they create meaningful systems of belonging. These practices align with folklore because they are informally learned, shared through participation, and adapted over time (Weston 1991).

Conclusion

Chosen-family traditions show that folklore is a living and flexible process. Through shared rituals and storytelling, LGBTQ+ communities actively create belonging and identity. These practices demonstrate how folklore is not only about preserving tradition but also about building meaning and connection in everyday life.
