Chosen Family as Contemporary Folklore

Exploring LGBTQ+ Rituals, Identity, and Community

Home Research Article Summary Bibliography

Abstract

This project examines chosen family in LGBTQ+ communities as contemporary folklore. It focuses on informal rituals such as Friendsgiving, coming-out celebrations, and name-change gatherings that create belonging. These traditions are learned through participation, repeated, and adapted over time. By analyzing their forms, meanings, and contexts, this research shows how chosen-family practices reflect values of care, resilience, and identity while redefining kinship and building community.

Research

Chosen-family rituals are customary folklore because they are informal, repeated, and meaningful. Events like Friendsgiving and transition celebrations involve shared meals, symbolic acts, and emotional expression that reinforce belonging and identity (Weston 1991).

These practices occur in homes, LGBTQ+ centers, and online. The flexibility of these spaces allows traditions to adapt while maintaining their purpose of building community and connection (Browne 2006).

Language and storytelling are central to chosen-family identity. Terms such as "chosen family" shape belonging, while shared narratives reinforce values and collective experience (Bauman 1977).

Article Summary

Kath Weston’s work explains how LGBTQ+ individuals form chosen families outside traditional kinship structures. These relationships are based on care, shared experience, and repeated practices. Rather than replacements for biological families, they create meaningful systems of belonging. These practices align with folklore because they are learned informally and expressed through participation (Weston 1991).
